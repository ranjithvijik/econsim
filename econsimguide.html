💡 Best Practice: The Ceteris Paribus Method
The Latin phrase ceteris paribus means "all else being equal." This is the fundamental method of economic analysis:
  • Change only ONE variable at a time
  • Observe the effect on equilibrium outcomes
  • Record the direction and magnitude of change
  • Only then move to the next variable
Do NOT change all sliders at once. Economics is easier to learn when you can trace one cause to one effect. As OpenStax notes: "Math is one tool, but it is not the only tool or even always the best tool economists can use."
⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes
  • Confusing movement along vs. shift of curves — Price changes cause movement ALONG; other factors cause SHIFTS
  • Ignoring units — Always check whether values are dollars, percentages, or index numbers
  • Forgetting time horizons — Short-run and long-run effects often differ dramatically
  • Assuming linear relationships — Many economic relationships are non-linear at extremes
  • Confusing correlation with causation — The simulator shows causal relationships; real data often shows correlation

📈 Supply & Demand Simulator

Find equilibrium, consumer surplus, producer surplus, and total surplus.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.3
Property Details
Difficulty Introductory
Chart Type Supply (green), demand (red), equilibrium (gold star)
Key Concepts Equilibrium price and quantity, surplus, shortage, welfare

Inputs / Controls

Input Meaning Typical Effect
Demand Intercept (a) Maximum willingness to pay Higher a shifts demand right
Demand Slope (b) Demand sensitivity to price Higher b means steeper demand
Supply Intercept (c) Minimum supply threshold Higher c shifts supply left/up

Core Formulas
Demand: Qd = a - b×P Supply: Qs = c + d×P
P* = (a − c) / (b + d)
Q* = a − b×P*
CS = ½ × (a/b − P*) × Q*
PS = ½ × (P* − c/d) × Q*
Start at default values and identify the equilibrium star.
Raise demand intercept to simulate higher income or stronger tastes.
Raise supply intercept to simulate higher production costs.
Compare CS and PS as equilibrium shifts.
⚠️ Common Mistake
Changing price causes movement along a curve. Changing any determinant causes the entire curve to shift. As OpenStax emphasizes: "Demand refers to the entire relationship between price and quantity — the whole curve."
📗 CLEAR IT UP: Demand vs. Quantity Demanded
Demand = the entire curve (relationship). Quantity demanded = a specific point on that curve at a given price. When we say "demand increases," the entire curve shifts right.
🔗 Five Demand Shifters
1. Income (normal vs inferior goods)
2. Tastes/Preferences
3. Price of Related Goods (substitutes & complements)
4. Expectations of future prices
5. Number of Buyers
🔗 Six Supply Shifters
1. Input Costs
2. Technology
3. Expectations
4. Number of Sellers
5. Natural Conditions
6. Government Policies (taxes/subsidies)
🏠 Bring It Home: Gasoline Markets
When oil prices rise (supply shifts left), equilibrium price increases and quantity decreases. When electric vehicles gain popularity (demand shifts left), both price and quantity fall.

🔵 Clear It Up: Does "Demand" Mean the Same as "Quantity Demanded"?

No! This is the #1 most common mistake in introductory economics:

  • Demand refers to the entire relationship between price and quantity — the whole curve. When we say "demand increases," we mean the entire curve shifts right: at every price, consumers want more.
  • Quantity demanded is a specific point on that curve at a given price. When price changes, we move along the existing curve — that's a change in quantity demanded, not a change in demand.

Test yourself: If the price of coffee rises, does demand for coffee decrease? No! The quantity demanded decreases (movement along the curve), but demand itself — the curve — hasn't moved.

🔵 Clear It Up: Is Supply the Same as Quantity Supplied?

Same distinction applies! Supply is the entire curve showing the relationship between price and quantity supplied. Quantity supplied is a specific amount at a specific price.

Key insight: A change in the good's own price causes movement along the curve. A change in anything else (income, tastes, input costs, technology) causes the curve to shift.

🟢 Work It Out: Finding Equilibrium Step-by-Step

Problem: Given Qd = 100 − 2P and Qs = 10 + 3P, find equilibrium price and quantity.

Step 1: Set Qd = Qs (equilibrium condition)
100 − 2P = 10 + 3P

Step 2: Solve for P
100 − 10 = 3P + 2P → 90 = 5P → P* = 18

Step 3: Substitute P back to find Q
Q = 100 − 2(18) = 100 − 36 = 64
Check: Q = 10 + 3(18) = 10 + 54 = 64 ✓

Step 4: Calculate Consumer Surplus
Pmax (demand intercept) = 100/2 = 50
CS = ½ × (50 − 18) × 64 = ½ × 32 × 64 = $1,024

Step 5: Calculate Producer Surplus
Pmin (supply intercept) = −10/3 ≈ 0 (floor at 0)
PS = ½ × (18 − 0) × 64 = $576

Answer: P* = $18, Q* = 64 units, CS = $1,024, PS = $576, TS = $1,600

🟣 Bring It Home: Gasoline Price Fluctuations

Gasoline prices fluctuate due to both supply and demand shifts. Use the simulator to model these scenarios:

  • Summer driving season: Demand shifts right → P↑, Q↑ (raise a slider)
  • Hurricane disrupts refineries: Supply shifts left → P↑, Q↓ (raise c slider)
  • Electric vehicle adoption: Demand shifts left → P↓, Q↓ (lower a slider)
  • New fracking technology: Supply shifts right → P↓, Q↑ (lower c slider)

Notice that supply shifts and demand shifts have opposite effects on quantity but the same effect on price when shifting in the same direction.

🟣 Bring It Home: The Organic Food Market

From OpenStax: Organic food sales have grown dramatically as consumer preferences shifted toward healthier options — a rightward shift in demand. Meanwhile, more farmers converted to organic production — a rightward shift in supply.

Simulate it: Start with a=80, c=20. Then increase a to 120 (demand shift) and decrease c to 5 (supply shift). Notice Q rises dramatically while P changes modestly — matching real organic market data where quantity grew faster than prices.

📐 Elasticity & Lerner Index

Measure responsiveness and connect elasticity to monopoly pricing power.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.5
Elasticity Zone Condition Total Revenue Rule Examples
Elastic |Ed| > 1 P↑ → TR↓ Luxury goods, dining out
Unit Elastic |Ed| = 1 TR unchanged Midpoint benchmark
Inelastic |Ed| < 1 P↑ → TR↑ Gasoline, insulin
Formulas
Ed = (%ΔQd) / (%ΔP) Midpoint: Ed = [(Q2-Q1)/((Q2+Q1)/2)] / [(P2-P1)/((P2+P1)/2)] Lerner Index: L = (P − MC)/P = 1/|ε| Optimal Markup: P* = MC × |ε|/(|ε| - 1)
📗 CLEAR IT UP: Elasticity ≠ Slope
Slope measures absolute change (ΔQ/ΔP). Elasticity measures percentage change (%ΔQ/%ΔP). Elasticity is unit-free — you can compare across different markets.
📗 Five Determinants of Elasticity
1. Availability of Substitutes — more substitutes = more elastic
2. Necessities vs. Luxuries — necessities = inelastic
3. Share of Budget — small share = inelastic
4. Time Horizon — short run = inelastic
5. Market Definition — narrow = elastic
💊 Pharma vs Commodities
Pharmaceuticals: |ε| ≈ 1.3 → Lerner = 0.77, markup = 333%
Wheat: |ε| ≈ 4 → Lerner = 0.25, markup = 33%

⚙️ Elasticity |ε| · ⚙️ Marginal Cost

Zone Condition TR Rule Business Strategy Examples
Perfectly Elastic |Ed| = ∞ Any P↑ → Q=0 Cannot raise price at all Commodities in perfect competition
Elastic |Ed| > 1 P↑ → TR↓ Cut prices to increase revenue Luxury goods, restaurant meals, airline tickets
Unit Elastic |Ed| = 1 TR unchanged Price changes don't affect revenue Theoretical midpoint

Perfectly Inelastic |Ed| = 0 Q unchanged Charge any price Life-saving medications with no substitutes
🔵 Clear It Up: Is Elasticity the Same as Slope?

No! This is a crucial distinction:

  • Slope measures absolute change (ΔQ/ΔP) — depends on the units used
  • Elasticity measures percentage change (%ΔQ/%ΔP) — unit-free ratio

Why this matters: Elasticity is unit-free, so you can compare across completely different markets (gasoline vs. healthcare vs. smartphones), while slopes depend on the units used and cannot be compared across markets.

Key insight: Along a linear demand curve with constant slope, elasticity varies — from infinite at the price intercept to zero at the quantity intercept. The midpoint has unit elasticity.

🟢 Work It Out: The Total Revenue Test

Problem: A movie theater raises ticket prices from $10 to $12. Attendance falls from 500 to 400 per week. Is demand elastic or inelastic? What happened to total revenue?

Step 1: Calculate elasticity using midpoint method
%ΔQ = (400−500)/[(400+500)/2] = −100/450 = −22.2%
%ΔP = (12−10)/[(12+10)/2] = 2/11 = 18.2%
|Ed| = 22.2% / 18.2% = 1.22

Step 2: Interpret
|Ed| = 1.22 > 1, so demand is elastic

Step 3: Check total revenue
Before: TR = $10 × 500 = $5,000
After: TR = $12 × 400 = $4,800
TR fell by $200

Conclusion: Because demand is elastic, the price increase reduced total revenue. The theater should consider lowering prices instead.

🟣 Bring It Home: Pharma vs. Commodities

The Lerner Index explains why some industries have enormous profit margins while others barely break even:

  • Pharmaceuticals: |ε| ≈ 1.3 → Lerner = 0.77 → Markup = 333% over MC
    Why? Few substitutes, life-saving necessity, patent protection
  • Wheat (commodity): |ε| ≈ 4 → Lerner = 0.25 → Markup = 33% over MC
    Why? Many substitutes, homogeneous product, many sellers

This is why drug companies earn high margins while farmers earn thin ones — it's all about elasticity.

🟣 Bring It Home: That Yoga Class

A yoga studio considers raising class prices from $10 to $15. Currently 30 students attend per class.

  • If demand is inelastic (|ε|=0.5): Attendance drops to ~23. Revenue = $345 (up from $300). Raise price ✓
  • If demand is elastic (|ε|=2.0): Attendance drops to ~15. Revenue = $225 (down from $300). Don't raise price ✗

The studio's decision depends entirely on elasticity — which is why businesses invest heavily in market research before changing prices.

🔗 Connection to Monopoly Pricing (Module 05)
The Lerner Index directly connects elasticity to market power. A monopolist facing inelastic demand can charge a much higher markup over marginal cost. The formula P* = MC × |ε|/(|ε|−1) shows that as elasticity approaches 1, the optimal price approaches infinity — which is why monopolists never operate on the inelastic portion of the demand curve.
Module P3 · OpenStax Micro Ch.7

💲 Cost Curves (TC, MC, ATC, AVC)

Understand the relationship between average and marginal costs.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.7
Reading from the textbook — Microeconomics 3e, §§7.1–7.2

Each business tries to earn a profit. Total revenue is income from selling products (price × quantity sold). Total cost is what the firm pays to produce and sell. We distinguish explicit costs (actual payments like wages and rent) from implicit costs (opportunity cost of resources the firm already owns).

Accounting profit is total revenue minus explicit costs. Economic profit is total revenue minus total cost, including both explicit and implicit costs.

Production is the process a firm uses to transform inputs into outputs. This chapter explores the relationship between the quantity of output and the cost of producing it — summarized by the firm's production function.

Access for free at openstax.org. Excerpted from Principles of Microeconomics 3e, §§7.1–7.2 (CC BY 4.0). Full PDF: Microeconomics3e-WEB.pdf
Cost Relationships
TC = FC + VC ATC = TC/Q = AFC + AVC AFC = FC/Q (always declining) AVC = VC/Q MC = ΔTC/ΔQ
📗 Key Insight
MC intersects AVC and ATC at their minimum points. When MC < ATC, ATC is falling. When MC> ATC, ATC is rising. This is the "marginal pulls average" rule.
📗 Economies of Scale
Economies of scale: LRAC falls as output increases (specialization, bulk purchasing)
Constant returns: LRAC flat
Diseconomies of scale: LRAC rises (coordination problems, bureaucracy)

🏢 Monopoly

Single-firm market power, MR=MC output, and deadweight loss.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.9
Monopoly Logic
Demand: P = a − bQ MR = a − 2bQ (twice as steep as demand) Set MR = MC → find Q* Use demand curve to find monopoly price P* DWL = ½ × (Pm - MC) × (Qc - Qm)
Locate MR=MC to find profit-maximizing quantity.
Move up to demand to read monopoly price.
Compare with competitive output where P=MC.
Identify DWL between monopoly and competitive quantities.
📗 Key Insight
MR lies below demand because the monopolist must lower price on all units to sell one more unit.
📗 CLEAR IT UP: Zero Economic Profit ≠ Going Broke
Zero economic profit includes opportunity cost. The firm earns a normal return — exactly what the owner could earn elsewhere. This is the long-run competitive equilibrium.

🔵 Clear It Up: Why Is MR Below Demand?

For a monopolist, MR < P because to sell one more unit, the firm must lower the price on all units, not just the marginal one.

Example: Currently selling 10 units at $50 each → TR = $500. To sell 11 units, must lower price to $48. New TR = $48 × 11 = $528. MR of 11th unit = $528 − $500 = $28, which is less than the $48 price.

The MR has two components: (1) gain from selling one more unit at the new price, minus (2) loss from selling all previous units at the lower price.

🔵 Clear It Up: Zero Economic Profit ≠ Going Broke

This confuses many students! Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit.

Economic profit includes the opportunity cost of the owner's time and capital. When economic profit = 0, the firm is earning a normal return — exactly what the owner could earn in their next best alternative.

Example: A restaurant owner earns $80,000/year in accounting profit. If they could earn $80,000 as a corporate chef, their economic profit is $0. They're indifferent between running the restaurant and being a chef — which is exactly the long-run competitive equilibrium.

🟢 Work It Out: Monopoly vs. Perfect Competition

Given: Demand P = 100 − Q, MC = ATC = $20 (constant)

Find Monopoly Outcomes:
MR = 100 − 2Q
Set MR = MC: 100 − 2Q = 20 → Qm = 40
Pm = 100 − 40 = $60
Profit = (60 − 20) × 40 = $1,600

Find Competitive Outcomes:
Set P = MC: 100 − Q = 20 → Qc = 80
Pc = $20 | Profit = $0 (normal return)

Calculate DWL:
DWL = ½ × (Pm − MC) × (Qc − Qm) = ½ × 40 × 40 = $800

Interpretation: Monopoly produces half the competitive quantity, charges triple the competitive markup, earns $1,600 profit, and destroys $800 of potential surplus.

🚧 Barriers to Entry: Sources of Monopoly Power
  1. Legal barriers — Patents, copyrights, licenses, franchises
  2. Control of essential resources — De Beers and diamonds (historically)
  3. Economies of scale — Natural monopoly: one firm can serve the market at lower cost than multiple firms
  4. Network effects — Value increases with users (Facebook, Windows)
  5. Strategic barriers — Predatory pricing, excess capacity, brand loyalty
🟣 Bring It Home: The Pharmaceutical Industry

Drug patents create temporary monopolies, allowing companies to charge prices far above marginal cost. A pill that costs $0.10 to produce might sell for $10 — a 10,000% markup.

The tradeoff: Without patent protection, firms wouldn't invest billions in R&D. But during the patent period, patients face high prices and some are priced out of the market (DWL).

Simulate it: Set MC = 10, demand intercept = 200. Compare monopoly price (~$105) to competitive price ($10). The DWL triangle represents patients who would benefit from the drug but can't afford it at the monopoly price.

Module 06 · Perloff & Brander Ch.13

📗 CLEAR IT UP: What IS a Nash Equilibrium?
A Nash Equilibrium is a set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. It does NOT mean the best outcome for everyone — just a stable resting point.

Perloff Ch.13
📘 Backward Induction Method
Step 1: Start at terminal nodes
Step 2: Determine optimal choice at each decision node
Step 3: Work backward to the root

✓ Credible Threats
Fighting is not credible if the incumbent gets -1 from fighting vs +2 from accommodating. Rational entrants see through the bluff. Commitment devices (sunk costs) make threats credible.
📘 Finite Repeated Games Unravel
When a game is repeated a known finite number of times T, backward induction unravels cooperation completely. Both firms know there's no punishment in period T, so they defect. Working backwards, they defect in every period.

📊 AD-AS Model

Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply: the workhorse short-run macro model.

OpenStax Macro 3e Ch.11
Reading from the textbook — Macroeconomics 3e, §§11.1–11.2

Macroeconomists divide between those who stress supply and those who stress demand. Say's law ("Supply creates its own demand") captures the supply-side view; over long horizons, as productive capacity grows, demand tends to keep pace. Keynes' law ("Demand creates its own supply") fits many short-run episodes when output is demand-constrained.

Neither story is complete alone: limits on labor, capital, and technology still bind no matter how high demand runs. The aggregate demand/aggregate supply model combines both. Aggregate supply is total real GDP firms will produce at each price level. Potential GDP is what the economy can produce at full employment. The price level is an index; the inflation rate is its percentage change over time.

Access for free at openstax.org. Excerpted from Principles of Macroeconomics 3e, §§11.1–11.2 (CC BY 4.0). Full PDF: Macroeconomics3e-WEB.pdf
Input Meaning Effect of Increase

Government Spending Fiscal stimulus AD shifts right
Money Supply Nominal money stock AD shifts right via lower rates
Input Cost Production cost SRAS shifts left
Supply Shock Temporary shock Positive → SRAS left
Potential Output (Yp) Full-employment GDP Moves LRAS
Conceptual Logic
AD: total planned spending at each price level (slopes down) SRAS: output firms produce at each price level (slopes up) LRAS: full-employment output (vertical at Yp) Recessionary gap: Y < Yp Inflationary gap: Y> Yp
📗 Why AD Slopes Downward
1. Wealth Effect: Higher P → lower real wealth → less spending
2. Interest Rate Effect: Higher P → higher money demand → higher rates → less investment
3. Exchange Rate Effect: Higher P → less competitive exports
⚠️ Stagflation Warning
A negative supply shock causes both higher inflation AND lower output. Demand-side policy alone cannot fix both simultaneously.

🔵 Clear It Up: Why Does AD Slope Downward?

The AD curve slopes downward for three distinct reasons — NOT the same as why individual demand curves slope down!

  1. Wealth Effect (Pigou Effect): Higher price level → real value of savings falls → consumers feel poorer → spend less
  2. Interest Rate Effect (Keynes Effect): Higher price level → need more money for transactions → interest rates rise → investment falls
  3. Exchange Rate Effect: Higher price level → domestic goods more expensive relative to foreign → exports fall, imports rise → net exports fall

All three effects mean: higher price level → lower real GDP demanded.

📊 Three Zones of SRAS (from OpenStax)
  • Keynesian Zone (far left): Economy well below potential. SRAS nearly horizontal — increases in AD raise output with little inflation. Fiscal/monetary stimulus is highly effective.
  • Intermediate Zone (middle): Economy approaching potential. AD increases raise both output and prices. Policy tradeoffs emerge.
  • Neoclassical Zone (far right): Economy at or above potential. SRAS nearly vertical — AD increases mainly raise prices with little output gain. Stimulus causes inflation, not growth.
⚠️ Stagflation Warning
A negative supply shock (SRAS shifts left) causes both higher inflation AND lower output simultaneously — this is called stagflation, the worst of both worlds. Demand-side policy alone cannot fix both problems:
  • Expansionary policy fixes output but worsens inflation
  • Contractionary policy fixes inflation but worsens output
This is why the 1970s oil shocks were so devastating — policymakers faced an impossible tradeoff.
🟢 Work It Out: Analyzing a Recessionary Gap

Scenario: The economy is at Y = $18 trillion, but potential GDP Yp = $20 trillion. Price level = 100.

Step 1: Identify the gap
Output gap = Y − Yp = $18T − $20T = −$2 trillion (recessionary gap)

Step 2: Policy options
Fiscal: Increase G or cut T to shift AD right by $2T
Monetary: Increase money supply to lower interest rates → shift AD right

Step 3: Self-correction (without policy)
High unemployment → workers accept lower wages → SRAS shifts right → output rises back to Yp
But: This process can take years — the "long run" Keynes warned about

Policy conclusion: If the gap is large and unemployment is causing hardship, active stabilization policy may be preferred over waiting for self-correction.

🟣 Bring It Home: The COVID-19 Dual Shock (2020)

The COVID-19 pandemic was both a negative demand shock (lockdowns reduced consumer spending, investment, and services demand) AND a negative supply shock (factory closures, supply chain disruptions, worker illness).

Simulate it: Simultaneously decrease Autonomous Spending (AD shifts left) and increase Input Cost (SRAS shifts left). Notice the economy experiences a severe output drop with possible stagflationary pressure.

The government responded with massive fiscal stimulus ($2.2 trillion CARES Act) to shift AD right, while the Fed cut rates to near zero. This explains why inflation surged in 2021-2022 as supply remained constrained but demand recovered rapidly.

🟣 Bring It Home: Historical Recessionary Episodes
  • 2008-09 Financial Crisis: AD collapsed (demand shock) → recessionary gap of ~$1T → Fed/Treasury responded with QE + TARP
  • 1970s Stagflation: Oil price shocks → SRAS shifted left → stagflation. Fed's contractionary response caused deep 1981-82 recession but broke inflation expectations
  • 1990s "Great Moderation": Stable AD growth + positive supply shocks (technology) → output above trend with low inflation (inflationary zone, managed well)

📗 Why Tax Multiplier < Spending Multiplier
When government spends $1, the entire dollar enters spending stream. When government cuts taxes by $1, consumers only spend MPC×$1 (they save the rest). With MPC=0.8: Spending mult=5, Tax mult=4.

Textbook multiplier overstates because it ignores: (1) Taxes on each round, (2) Import leakage, (3) Crowding out, (4) Time lags, (5) Ricardian equivalence.

✖️ Keynesian Cross

45-degree line model of aggregate expenditure and equilibrium output.

OpenStax Macro 3e Ch.12
Reading from the textbook — Macroeconomics 3e, §12.1

The Keynesian perspective focuses on aggregate demand. While factors of production determine potential GDP, real GDP depends on how much demand exists. If AD falls from full employment, the economy can sit in a recessionary gap; if AD pushes beyond potential, an inflationary gap and inflation can result. Policy may aim to close those gaps.

Aggregate demand is total planned spending on domestic goods and services — the sum of consumption, investment, government spending, and net exports.

Access for free at openstax.org. Excerpted from Principles of Macroeconomics 3e, §12.1 (CC BY 4.0). Full PDF: Macroeconomics3e-WEB.pdf
Keynesian Cross
AE = C₀ + MPC·Y + I + G Equilibrium: Y* = (C₀ + I + G)/(1−MPC) Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC)
Find AE at each level of Y.
Locate 45-degree line where AE = Y.
Identify equilibrium at the intersection.
📗 Why the 45° Line Matters
The 45° line represents all points where planned expenditure equals actual output (AE = Y). Above the line: inventories fall → firms increase production. Below: inventories pile up → firms cut production.

💵 Money Multiplier

How bank lending expands deposits and the money supply.

OpenStax Macro 3e Ch.14
Money Multiplier
m = 1 / RR Total money = initial deposit × m Example: RR = 10% → m = 10 $1,000 deposit → $10,000 total money supply
📗 Interpretation
The lower the reserve requirement, the larger the theoretical money multiplier. Banks "create" money through lending — but they create liquidity, not wealth.
🏠 Fed's 0% Reserve Requirement (2020)
In March 2020, the Fed reduced RR to 0%. Theoretical multiplier = infinite. In practice, banks still hold voluntary reserves due to operational needs and interest on reserves (IOR).

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.7
📗 Three Stages of Production
Stage I: MP > AP, AP rising. Adding workers increases average productivity.
Stage II: MP < AP but MP> 0. Rational production zone.
Stage III: MP < 0. Adding workers reduces total output (overcrowding).
🏠 The Kitchen Analogy
1 chef: Does everything alone — slow but functional (Stage I)
2-3 chefs: Specialization kicks in (Stage I → II)
4-6 chefs: Still productive but bumping into each other (Stage II)
10+ chefs: Kitchen overcrowded, total output may fall (Stage III)

Module I1 · OpenStax Micro Ch.16

🔍 Asymmetric Information

Lemons, adverse selection, moral hazard, and signaling.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.16
📗 Market for Lemons (Akerlof)
Buyers can't distinguish quality. They offer average-quality price. High-quality sellers exit. Average quality falls. Price falls. More sellers exit. Market collapses to only "lemons."
📗 Adverse Selection vs. Moral Hazard
Adverse Selection: Hidden information BEFORE transaction (insurance attracts high-risk buyers)
Moral Hazard: Hidden action AFTER transaction (insured people take more risks)
📗 Solutions
Signaling: Informed party sends costly signal (education, warranties)
Screening: Uninformed party designs menu to separate types (deductibles)

🏛️ Public Goods & Free Rider

Why private markets underprovide public goods and how governments respond.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.13
Good Type Excludable Rival Example
Private Good Yes Yes Food, shoes
Public Good No No National defense, lighthouse
Club Good Yes No Netflix, toll road
Common Resource No Yes Fish stocks, groundwater
⚠️ Free Rider Problem
If people can benefit without paying, each individual has an incentive to withhold payment — so the market underprovides or fails entirely. This is why governments typically provide public goods.
📗 Tragedy of the Commons
Common resources face overuse because no one owns them. Each user captures full benefit but bears only a fraction of depletion cost. Solutions: property rights, quotas, community management.
