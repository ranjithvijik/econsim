📘 From Perloff & Brander Ch.7 — Present Value of the Firm

A firm's value equals the present value of its future profit stream: PV = π₁/(1+i) + π₂/(1+i)² + π₃/(1+i)³ + ... For a stock paying constant growing dividends, this simplifies to the Gordon Growth Model: PV = D₀(1+g)/(r-g), where D₀ is the current dividend, g is the growth rate, and r is the discount rate. The discount rate should reflect the opportunity cost of capital — which is linked to the Fed Funds rate, risk premium, and the firm's beta. 📗 CLEAR IT UP: Does Demand Mean the Same as Quantity Demanded?

No! Demand refers to the entire relationship between price and quantity — the whole curve. Quantity demanded is a specific point on that curve at a given price. When we say "demand increases," we mean the entire curve shifts right (at every price, consumers want more). When price changes, we move along the existing curve — that's a change in quantity demanded, not a change in demand. Try it: Increase the Demand Intercept (a) slider above — this shifts the entire demand curve right (increase in demand). Now move the Supply Intercept (c) — notice how the equilibrium point moves along both curves.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Is Supply the Same as Quantity Supplied?

Same distinction applies to supply. Supply is the entire curve; quantity supplied is a specific amount at a specific price. Factors that shift supply include: input costs, technology, expectations, number of sellers, and natural conditions. Key insight: A change in the good's own price causes movement along the curve. A change in anything else (income, tastes, input costs, technology) causes the curve to shift.

📗 WORK IT OUT: Finding Equilibrium Step-by-Step

Step 1: Write the demand equation: Qd = a - b×P Step 2: Write the supply equation: Qs = c + d×P Step 3: Set Qd = Qs (equilibrium condition) Step 4: Solve for P*: a - b×P = c + d×P → P* = (a - c)/(b + d) Step 5: Substitute P* back into either equation to find Q* Step 6: Calculate CS = ½ × (max willingness to pay - P*) × Q* Step 7: Calculate PS = ½ × P* × Q* The sliders above perform these calculations in real-time. Adjust any parameter and watch the equilibrium recalculate instantly.

Price Elasticity of Demand quantifies how hyper-responsive consumers are to price fluctuations, forming the bedrock of managerial strategy. 📘 Perloff & Brander 3e · Ch.3 📗 OpenStax Micro 3e · Ch.5 Elasticity
Lerner Index & Optimal Pricing

L = 1/|ε| P* = MC × |ε|/(|ε| - 1) Markup = 1/(|ε| - 1)

💊 Pharma vs Commodities

Pharmaceuticals face inelastic demand (|ε| ≈ 1.3) → Lerner = 0.77, markup = 333%. Wheat faces elastic demand (|ε| ≈ 4) → Lerner = 0.25, markup = 33%. This explains why drug companies earn high margins while farmers earn thin ones.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Is Elasticity the Same as Slope?

No! Slope measures the absolute change (ΔQ/ΔP), while elasticity measures the percentage change (%ΔQ/%ΔP). This matters because elasticity is unit-free — you can compare elasticity across completely different markets (gasoline vs. healthcare vs. smartphones), while slopes depend on the units used. A steep demand curve can still be elastic if the price level is high relative to quantity. The midpoint method avoids the problem of getting different elasticities depending on direction: Midpoint Formula: Ed = [(Q2-Q1)/((Q2+Q1)/2)] / [(P2-P1)/((P2+P1)/2)]

📗 WORK IT OUT: The Total Revenue Test

The Total Revenue Test is the quickest way to determine elasticity: If price↑ and TR↑: Demand is inelastic (|ε| < 1). The price effect dominates. If price↑ and TR↓: Demand is elastic (|ε| > 1). The quantity effect dominates. If price↑ and TR unchanged: Demand is unit elastic (|ε| = 1). Business application: If your product has elastic demand, never raise prices — you'll lose more revenue from lost customers than you gain from higher prices. If inelastic, a price increase boosts revenue. Try setting |ε| = 1.5 (elastic) above: the optimal markup is only 200%. Now set |ε| = 5 (very elastic): markup drops to just 25%. This is why commodity producers (wheat, oil) have thin margins while luxury brands (Louis Vuitton) have enormous margins.

📗 FIVE DETERMINANTS OF ELASTICITY

1. Availability of Substitutes: More substitutes → more elastic. Insulin has no substitutes (very inelastic). Pepsi has many (very elastic). 2. Necessities vs. Luxuries: Necessities (food, medicine) → inelastic. Luxuries (vacations, jewelry) → elastic. 3. Share of Budget: Small share (salt, matches) → inelastic. Large share (housing, cars) → elastic. 4. Time Horizon: Short run → inelastic (can't adjust). Long run → elastic (can find alternatives). 5. Market Definition: Narrow definition (Coca-Cola) → elastic. Broad definition (beverages) → inelastic.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Diminishing Marginal Returns vs. Negative Returns

Diminishing marginal returns means each additional worker adds less output than the previous one — but still adds something positive. Total output is still rising, just at a decreasing rate. Negative returns means adding another worker actually reduces total output — workers are getting in each other's way. No rational firm operates here. 📗 WORK IT OUT: Why MC Crosses ATC at Its Minimum

Think of it like your GPA: If your next grade (marginal) is higher than your current GPA (average), your GPA rises. If your next grade is lower than your current GPA, your GPA falls. Therefore, MC = ATC at the exact minimum of ATC. This is the efficient scale — the output level where average cost is minimized. In the simulator, this is calculated as Q* = √(FC/QC). In perfect competition, long-run equilibrium forces firms to produce at this exact point.

📗 ECONOMIES OF SCALE: The Long-Run Perspective

Economies of scale: As output increases, LRAC falls. Sources: specialization, bulk purchasing, spreading fixed costs. Constant returns to scale: LRAC is flat. Doubling inputs exactly doubles output. Diseconomies of scale: LRAC rises. Sources: coordination problems, bureaucracy, communication breakdowns. The minimum efficient scale (MES) is the smallest output at which LRAC is minimized. Industries with high MES (automobiles, aircraft) tend to be oligopolies. Industries with low MES (restaurants, hair salons) tend to be competitive.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Zero Economic Profit ≠ Going Broke

This is one of the most common confusions in economics! Zero economic profit ≠ zero accounting profit. Economic profit includes the opportunity cost of the owner's time and capital. When economic profit = 0, the firm is earning a normal return — exactly what the owner could earn in their next best alternative. Example: A restaurant owner earns $80,000/year in accounting profit. If they could earn $80,000 as a corporate chef, their economic profit is $0. They're indifferent between running the restaurant and being a chef — which is exactly the long-run competitive equilibrium.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: What Exactly IS a Nash Equilibrium?

A Nash Equilibrium is a set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, given what the other players are doing. It does NOT mean: ✗ The best possible outcome for everyone (that's Pareto optimal) ✗ The outcome players would choose if they could cooperate ✗ A unique outcome (there can be multiple Nash equilibria) It DOES mean: ✓ A stable resting point — once reached, no one wants to deviate ✓ A self-enforcing agreement — no external enforcement needed How to find it: For each cell in the payoff matrix, check: could Player 1 do better by switching rows (holding P2's column fixed)? Could Player 2 do better by switching columns (holding P1's row fixed)? If neither can improve, it's a Nash Equilibrium.

📗 WORK IT OUT: Solving the Prisoner's Dilemma

Step 1: Check P1's best response to P2 playing Left: Up gives 3, Down gives 5 → Down is better Step 2: Check P1's best response to P2 playing Right: Up gives 0, Down gives 1 → Down is better Step 3: Down is better regardless of P2's choice → Down is P1's dominant strategy Step 4: By symmetry, Right is P2's dominant strategy Step 5: Nash Equilibrium = (Down, Right) = (1, 1) Step 6: But (Up, Left) = (3, 3) is Pareto superior! Both players would prefer cooperation, but individual rationality prevents it. This is the fundamental tension in oligopoly: firms would earn more by colluding, but each has an incentive to cheat.

Coordination Games

Unlike hyper-competitive zero-sum games, coordination games feature multiple equally optimal Nash equilibria where the sole challenge is achieving systemic alignment. From standardizing EV charging ports and 5G cellular frequencies to the global rollout of Bluetooth (an SSO involving thousands of firms), the inability to coordinate results in catastrophic network fragmentation. ✓ Pareto Criterion

Rational players coordinate on the Pareto-dominant equilibrium (A,A) = (3,3) over (B,B) = (2,2).

🎯 Focal Points (Schelling)

Cultural, historical, or salient features make some equilibria "focal." Meeting at noon, driving on the right — conventions emerge without explicit coordination.

📋 Standard Setting Organizations

When focal points aren't enough, firms create formal coordination mechanisms.

"Smartphones use the Bluetooth standard, as do many car audio systems, headphones, and hearing aids. How did so many different companies agree to coordinate on the Bluetooth standard?" Answer: The Bluetooth Special Interest Group, a not-for-profit SSO with over 33,000 member companies as of 2018. SSOs solve coordination games by: (1) establishing a common standard, (2) requiring patent-holders to license technology on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) terms, and (3) providing a forum for pre-competitive collaboration. Without SSOs, firms might get stuck on incompatible standards — the (0,0) outcome.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Coordination Games Beyond Bluetooth

Coordination games arise whenever players benefit from choosing the same strategy, but there are multiple equilibria and no obvious way to coordinate: 1. Technology Standards (Battle of the Sexes): • VHS vs Betamax (1980s): Both formats worked, but consumers needed to coordinate on one. VHS won despite Betamax being technically superior — because JVC licensed VHS freely while Sony restricted Betamax. • USB-C vs Lightning (2023): EU mandated USB-C, forcing coordination by regulation. 2. Driving Side (Pure Coordination): • Left or right — doesn't matter which, as long as everyone agrees. Sweden switched from left to right on September 3, 1967 ("Dagen H"). The transition required massive coordination. 3. Network Effects (Stag Hunt): • Social media: Everyone benefits from being on the same platform. Facebook beat MySpace not because it was better, but because it reached critical mass first. 4. Bank Runs (Coordination Failure): • If everyone believes the bank is solvent → bank IS solvent (good equilibrium). • If everyone believes the bank will fail → everyone withdraws → bank DOES fail (bad equilibrium). Key insight: In coordination games, the equilibrium that gets selected often depends on focal points (Schelling, 1960), history (path dependence), or regulation (government mandates).

🎓 SCHELLING FOCAL POINTS

Thomas Schelling's famous experiment: "You are to meet someone in New York City tomorrow. You have not been able to communicate beforehand. Where and when do you go?" Most people answer: Grand Central Station, noon. There's nothing special about this answer — it's a focal point, a solution that seems "natural" or "obvious" to most people. Focal points help solve coordination problems without communication. They arise from shared culture, convention, or salience.

Unregulated free markets frequently hallucinate true societal costs, triggering profound Market Failures. Conversely, positive externalities (open-source software, vaccination networks) face chronic underfunding. ⚖️ Coase Theorem

If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are low, private bargaining can achieve efficiency regardless of the initial allocation of rights. The assignment of rights affects distribution but not efficiency.

📗 From OpenStax Micro 3e — Policy Solutions Compared

1. Pigouvian Tax: Tax = MEC. Internalizes the externality. Efficient but requires knowing the exact MEC. 2. Cap-and-Trade: Set total pollution cap, issue tradeable permits. A steel mill emits SO₂ that damages nearby crops. The farmer bears $15/ton in crop losses (external cost). Without intervention, the mill produces 80 tons (market Q). With a $15/ton Pigouvian tax, the mill reduces to 65 tons (optimal Q). The DWL triangle = ½ × 15 × 15 = $112.50 is eliminated. Try setting EC = $15 above to see this.

🎓 Positive Externality: Education

Education benefits not just the student (higher wages) but society (lower crime, better civic participation, innovation spillovers). MSB > MPB means the market underproduces education. Solutions: government subsidies, public schools, tax credits for tuition. This is why most countries subsidize education heavily — the social return exceeds the private return.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Negative vs. Positive Externalities

Negative externality: A cost imposed on third parties who are NOT part of the transaction. The market overproduces because producers don't bear the full social cost. Examples: pollution, noise, traffic congestion, secondhand smoke Solution: Tax the activity (Pigouvian tax) to internalize the external cost Positive externality: A benefit enjoyed by third parties who are NOT part of the transaction. The market underproduces because producers can't capture the full social benefit. Examples: education, vaccination, R&D, beekeeping near orchards Solution: Subsidize the activity to encourage more production Key formula: MSC = MPC + MEC (for negative externalities) MSB = MPB + MEB (for positive externalities)

Step 1: Identify the external cost per unit (EC = $15 in our example) Step 2: The optimal Pigouvian tax = EC = $15/unit Step 3: With the tax, the firm's effective MC rises from PMC to PMC + tax = SMC Step 4: The firm reduces output from Qmarket to Qoptimal Step 5: DWL is eliminated — the tax forces the firm to account for the full social cost Step 6: Tax revenue = tax × Qoptimal (can be used to compensate those harmed) Try it: Set PMC = $20 and EC = $15. 📗 FOUR POLICY APPROACHES COMPARED

1. Pigouvian Tax: Set tax = MEC. Efficient but requires knowing the exact external cost. Used for: carbon taxes, cigarette taxes, alcohol taxes. 2. Cap-and-Trade: Set total pollution cap, issue tradeable permits. Achieves target at lowest total cost because firms with low abatement costs reduce more. Used for: SO₂ (US Acid Rain Program), CO₂ (EU ETS). 3. Command-and-Control: Direct regulation (emission standards, technology mandates). Simple to enforce but inflexible. Used for: vehicle emission standards, building codes. 4. Coase Bargaining: Private negotiation between polluter and victim. Works when: few parties, low transaction costs, well-defined property rights. Fails for: diffuse pollution affecting millions.

🏠 BRING IT HOME: The Tragedy of the Commons

Common resources (fisheries, clean air, groundwater) face overuse because no one owns them. Each user captures the full benefit of using the resource but bears only a fraction of the cost of depletion. Example: A fishery can sustainably yield 100 tons/year. With 10 fishers, each catches 10 tons. But each fisher has an incentive to catch more — the benefit of an extra ton goes entirely to them, while the cost (depleted fish stock) is shared among all 10. Result: overfishing, stock collapse. Solutions: Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs), fishing licenses, seasonal closures, community management (Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize work).

📊 Revenue Equivalence Theorem

Under independent private values, risk-neutral bidders, and symmetric information: all four auction formats yield the same expected revenue to the seller. 📗 From OpenStax Macro 3e — Why the Real Multiplier is Smaller

The textbook multiplier 1/(1-MPC) overstates the real-world impact because it ignores: 1. Taxes: Each round of spending is taxed, reducing the amount re-spent. 📗 CLEAR IT UP: Why Tax Multiplier < Spending Multiplier

When the government spends $1, the entire dollar enters the spending stream immediately. When the government cuts taxes by $1, consumers only spend MPC × $1 of it (they save the rest). Spending multiplier: 1/(1-MPC) = 1/MPS Tax multiplier: -MPC/(1-MPC) = -MPC/MPS With MPC = 0.8: Spending mult = 5, Tax mult = -4. A $100B spending increase raises GDP by $500B. A $100B tax cut raises GDP by only $400B. Balanced Budget Multiplier: If ΔG = ΔT (equal increase in spending and taxes), the net multiplier = 1. GDP rises by exactly the amount of the spending increase.

📗 WORK IT OUT: The Multiplier Process Round by Round

Government spends $100B on infrastructure. MPC = 0.8: Round 1: Government spends $100B → construction workers earn $100B Round 2: Workers spend 80% = $80B → retailers earn $80B Round 3: Retailers spend 80% = $64B → suppliers earn $64B Round 4: Suppliers spend 80% = $51.2B → ... Round 5: $41.0B → ... Total: $100 + $80 + $64 + $51.2 + $41.0 + ... = $100 × 1/(1-0.8) = $500B Each round, 20% leaks out as savings. The geometric series converges to 1/(1-MPC) times the initial injection.

⚠️ WHY THE REAL MULTIPLIER IS SMALLER

The textbook multiplier 1/(1-MPC) overstates the real-world impact because it ignores: 1. Taxes: Each round of spending is taxed, reducing the amount re-spent. With tax rate t: multiplier = 1/(1-MPC(1-t)) 2. Imports: Some spending leaks to foreign goods. With import propensity m: multiplier = 1/(1-MPC+m) 3. Crowding Out: Government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment. Use the CO slider to simulate. 4. Time Lags: Recognition lag → implementation lag → impact lag. Policy often arrives too late. 5. Ricardian Equivalence: If consumers expect future tax increases to pay for current spending, they save more now, reducing the multiplier. Real-world estimates: spending multiplier ≈ 1.5-2.5 (not 5). Tax multiplier ≈ 0.5-1.5 (not 4).

Phillips Curve

The notorious short-run paradox between Inflation and Unemployment. The Phillips Curve brutally illustrates that policymakers attempting to aggressively overdrive output and employment below the Natural Rate inevitably trigger massive, self-sustaining inflationary spirals. Conversely, incorporating Rational Expectations demonstrates that the Long-Run Phillips Curve rests perfectly vertical—proving categorically that central banks cannot permanently purchase employment using the currency of inflation without devastating the purchasing power of the populace.

Solow Growth Model

The ultimate mathematical paradigm for predicting the multi-generational prosperity of nations. The Solow Neoclassical Growth Model proves that while brute capital accumulation engine eventually suffocates under the crushing weight of capital depreciation (the Steady State), true infinite exponential growth relies exclusively on the exogenous magic of Technological Progress. Calibrate the savings rate closer to the "Golden Rule" optimum to permanently elevate the standard of living and global economic dominance of populations.
📗 OpenStax Macro 3e · Ch.7 Economic Growth + Ch.19 Neoclassical
Solow Model Equations

y = A·kα (per-worker production) k* = (s·A/(δ+n))1/(1−α) (steady state) y* = A·(k*)α (steady-state output) c* = (1−s)·y* (steady-state consumption) Golden Rule: s = α (max consumption)

💡 Growth Insight

Adjust savings toward the Golden Rule.

📗 From OpenStax Macro 3e Ch.19 — Neoclassical Growth Consensus

The aggregate production function Y = f(K, L, T) shows that output depends on: Physical Capital (K): Machines, factories, infrastructure. Subject to diminishing returns — doubling capital less than doubles output. This is why capital accumulation alone cannot sustain growth. Human Capital (L): Education, skills, health of the workforce. A more educated worker produces more with the same capital. This explains why countries invest heavily in education. Technology (T): The only source of sustained long-run growth. Technology shifts the production function upward, allowing more output from the same inputs. Increase the "Technology Level (A)" slider above to see this effect — it raises both k* and y*. Key insight: In the Solow model, savings rate changes affect the level of output but not the long-run growth rate. Only technological progress drives sustained per-capita growth.

🏠 Bring It Home: Calories & Economic Growth

From OpenStax Macro 3e: "The dramatic rise in incomes allowed the average person to eat better and consume more calories." Before the Industrial Revolution, most humans lived at subsistence level (~2,000 calories/day). Modern economic growth — driven by technology (A) and capital accumulation (K) — raised living standards so dramatically that obesity, not starvation, became the health challenge in developed nations. Set A=0.5 to simulate pre-industrial economies, then increase to A=2.0 to see the transformation.

🌍 Convergence: Rich vs Poor Countries

The Solow model predicts conditional convergence: countries with similar savings rates, population growth, and technology should converge to similar income levels. Poor countries (low k) grow faster because they're further from steady state. But unconditional convergence doesn't hold — countries with different fundamentals converge to different steady states. Compare: s=0.30, n=0.01 (developed) vs s=0.10, n=0.04 (developing) to see the income gap.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Why Can't Capital Alone Sustain Growth?

This is the central insight of the Solow model: capital accumulation alone cannot sustain long-run growth because of diminishing returns. Each additional unit of capital adds less output than the previous one. Eventually, new investment just replaces depreciated capital (the "steady state"). At this point, growth in output per worker stops — unless there is technological progress. Technology (A) is the only source of sustained per-capita growth. It shifts the entire production function upward. Increase the "Technology Level (A)" slider to see this — both k* and y* rise. Key implication: Changes in the savings rate affect the level of output but NOT the long-run growth rate. Only technological progress drives sustained per-capita growth.

📗 WORK IT OUT: Finding the Steady State

Step 1: Write the production function: y = A·k^α Step 2: Investment per worker: i = s·y = s·A·k^α Step 3: Break-even investment: (δ+n)·k (replaces depreciation + equips new workers) Step 4: Steady state where i = break-even: s·A·k^α = (δ+n)·k Step 5: Solve for k*: k* = [s·A/(δ+n)]^(1/(1-α)) Step 6: Steady-state output: y* = A·(k*)^α Step 7: Steady-state consumption: c* = (1-s)·y* In the Solow diagram above, the steady state is where the green curve (s·f(k)) intersects the red line ((δ+n)·k). To the left of k*, investment exceeds break-even → k grows. To the right, break-even exceeds investment → k shrinks.

📗 THE GOLDEN RULE: MAXIMIZING CONSUMPTION

The Golden Rule savings rate maximizes steady-state consumption per worker. It occurs where: s* = α (savings rate equals capital's share of income) Below Golden Rule (s < α): Increasing savings raises both k* and c*. Society is "under-saving." Above Golden Rule (s > α): Increasing savings raises k* but lowers c*. Society is "over-saving" — dynamically inefficient. With α = 0.33, the Golden Rule savings rate is 33%. The US saves about 18% — well below the Golden Rule, suggesting Americans could benefit from saving more. Try it: Set α = 0.33 and adjust the savings rate. Watch how c* first rises then falls as s increases past 0.33.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Why Is the PPF Curved (Not Straight)?

A straight-line PPF implies constant opportunity cost — each unit of Good A always costs the same amount of Good B. This only happens when resources are perfectly adaptable between uses. A bowed-out (concave) PPF reflects increasing opportunity cost: as you produce more of Good A, you must give up increasingly larger amounts of Good B. Why? Because resources are NOT equally suited to producing both goods. The first units of A use resources best suited to A (low opportunity cost). Later units require diverting resources better suited to B (high opportunity cost). Example: Converting farmland to housing. The first acres converted are rocky, hilly land (low agricultural value → low opportunity cost). Later acres are prime farmland (high opportunity cost).

📗 THREE KEY CONCEPTS FROM THE PPF

1. Efficiency: Points ON the PPF are productively efficient — all resources are fully employed. Points INSIDE are inefficient (unemployment, waste). Points OUTSIDE are currently unattainable. 2. Opportunity Cost: The slope of the PPF at any point = the opportunity cost of Good A in terms of Good B. As you move along the curve, the slope gets steeper (increasing opportunity cost). 3. Economic Growth: The PPF shifts outward when: technology improves, resources increase, or productivity rises. Use the "Technology Boost" slider to see this shift. Growth doesn't have to be uniform — technology in one sector shifts the PPF asymmetrically.

KEYNESIAN 01

Keynesian Cross & Spending Multiplier

Determine equilibrium output where planned expenditure equals actual output. The spending multiplier amplifies changes in autonomous spending.
📗 OpenStax Macro 3e · Ch.12
Keynesian Cross

AE = C₀ + MPC·Y + I + GY* = AE / (1 - MPC)Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)

💡 Multiplier Effect

Each $1 of government spending creates $4 of total output through successive rounds of spending.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Why Does the 45° Line Matter?

The 45° line represents all points where planned expenditure equals actual output (AE = Y). At any point above the 45° line, planned spending exceeds output → inventories fall → firms increase production. Below the 45° line, output exceeds spending → inventories pile up → firms cut production. The economy naturally gravitates toward the intersection — the equilibrium. Try it: Increase Government Spending (G) by $100. The AE line shifts up by $100, but the new equilibrium Y increases by $100 × multiplier. With MPC = 0.75, that's $100 × 4 = $400!

📗 WORK IT OUT: Deriving the Multiplier

Step 1: AE = C₀ + MPC·Y + I + G (planned expenditure) Step 2: At equilibrium: Y = AE → Y = C₀ + MPC·Y + I + G Step 3: Y - MPC·Y = C₀ + I + G Step 4: Y(1 - MPC) = C₀ + I + G Step 5: Y* = (C₀ + I + G) / (1 - MPC) Step 6: Multiplier = 1/(1 - MPC) = 1/MPS With MPC = 0.75: Multiplier = 1/0.25 = 4. Every $1 of autonomous spending creates $4 of equilibrium output.

🏠 BRING IT HOME: The CARES Act (2020)

The $2.2 trillion CARES Act included $1,200 stimulus checks, enhanced unemployment benefits, and PPP loans. If MPC ≈ 0.7 and ΔG ≈ $1T of direct spending: ΔY = $1T × 1/(1-0.7) = $3.3T. But crowding out, import leakage, and savings behavior reduced the actual multiplier to ≈ 1.5-2.0. Simulate: Set C₀=200, MPC=0.70, I=150, G=300. Then increase G to 400 (+$100). Watch Y* jump by $333 (multiplier = 3.33).

KEYNESIAN 02

Paradox of Thrift

When everyone saves more, total output falls — potentially reducing total savings. A key Keynesian insight.
📗 OpenStax Macro 3e · Ch.12
🔄 Paradox

✓ Paradox confirmed: saving more → less output

📗 CLEAR IT UP: How Can Saving More Make Everyone Poorer?

This is the Paradox of Thrift — one of Keynes's most counterintuitive insights: Individual level: Saving more is rational and prudent. Aggregate level: If EVERYONE saves more simultaneously, total spending falls → firms cut production → workers lose income → total savings may actually FALL. This is a fallacy of composition — what's true for one person isn't true for the whole economy. When MPC falls (people save more), the multiplier shrinks: 1/(1-MPC) gets smaller. Try it: Set MPC = 0.80 (multiplier = 5). Then increase "Savings Increase" to 15%. Watch the multiplier fall and output decline.

📗 WHEN DOES THE PARADOX APPLY?

Applies during recessions: When the economy is below full employment, increased saving reduces demand without being channeled into productive investment. Does NOT apply at full employment: When the economy is at potential output, increased saving funds more investment → higher future growth (Solow model). The classical view holds in the long run. Key distinction: Short-run Keynesian analysis → paradox holds. Long-run neoclassical analysis → saving is beneficial.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: Do Banks Really "Create" Money?

Yes! Banks create money through lending. When you deposit $1,000, the bank keeps $100 as reserves (at 10% RR) and lends $900. That $900 gets deposited in another bank, which keeps $90 and lends $810. In March 2020, the Fed reduced the reserve requirement to 0%. The theoretical money multiplier became infinite (1/0 = ∞). In practice, banks still hold voluntary reserves because: (1) they need cash for daily operations, (2) the Fed pays interest on reserves (IOR), and (3) regulatory capital requirements still constrain lending. KEYNESIAN 04

Purchasing Power & Inflation Erosion

See how inflation erodes the real value of money over time.
📗 OpenStax Macro 3e · Ch.9
📗 CLEAR IT UP: Why Does Inflation Hurt Savers?

Inflation is a hidden tax on cash holdings. If you keep $10,000 under your mattress and inflation is 3%/year, after 10 years your money buys only $7,441 worth of goods — a 25.6% loss. Winners from inflation: Borrowers (repay with cheaper dollars), owners of real assets (property, stocks), workers with COLA adjustments. Losers from inflation: Savers, fixed-income retirees, creditors, anyone on a fixed nominal contract. Rule of 70: Purchasing power halves in 70/inflation rate years. At 3%: ~23 years. At 7% (like 2022): just 10 years!

📗 REAL vs NOMINAL INTEREST RATES

Fisher Equation: Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate If your savings account pays 4% but inflation is 3%, your real return is only 1%. If inflation exceeds the nominal rate, you're losing purchasing power despite earning "interest." This is why the Fed's interest rate decisions matter so much.

📗 CLEAR IT UP: How Is the Poverty Line Calculated?

The US poverty threshold was developed by Mollie Orshansky in 1963: cost of a minimum food diet × 3. Updated annually for inflation but the methodology hasn't changed in 60 years. Criticisms: Doesn't account for regional cost differences, non-cash benefits (SNAP, Medicaid), taxes/credits (EITC), or that food is now only ~13% of spending (not 33%). 📗 POVERTY DEPTH vs POVERTY RATE

The poverty rate tells you what percentage are below the line — but not how far below. Poverty depth measures the average shortfall. A family at $25,000 against $26,500 has depth of 5.7%. A family at $10,000 has depth of 62.3%. Policy implication: A program lifting many from $25K to $27K reduces the rate dramatically but doesn't help deep poverty. Targeting the poorest reduces depth but may not change the rate. Both metrics matter.
