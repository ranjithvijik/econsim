Module 01 · OpenStax Micro Ch.3

📈 Supply & Demand Simulator

Find equilibrium, consumer surplus, producer surplus, and total surplus.

OpenStax Micro 3e Ch.3
PropertyDetails
DifficultyIntroductory
Chart TypeSupply (green), demand (red), equilibrium (gold star)
Key ConceptsEquilibrium price and quantity, surplus, shortage, welfare

Inputs / Controls

InputMeaningTypical Effect
Demand Intercept (a)Maximum willingness to payHigher a shifts demand right
Demand Slope (b)Demand sensitivity to priceHigher b means steeper demand
Supply Intercept (c)Minimum supply thresholdHigher c shifts supply left/up
Supply Slope (d)Supply sensitivityHigher d means flatter supply in P-Q form
Core Formulas
P* = (a − c) / (b + d) Q* = a − bP* CS = 1/2 × (a/b − P*) × Q* PS = 1/2 × (P* − c/d) × Q*

How to Use

Start at default values and identify the equilibrium star.
Raise demand intercept to simulate higher income or stronger tastes.
Raise supply intercept to simulate higher production costs.
Compare CS and PS as equilibrium shifts.
⚠️ Common Mistake
Changing price causes movement along a curve. Changing any determinant causes the entire curve to shift.
📗 Textbook Connection
OpenStax Ch.3 emphasizes that demand and supply curves hold “all else equal.” That assumption is exactly what the sliders let you relax one variable at a time.

📐 Elasticity & Lerner Index

Measure responsiveness and connect elasticity to monopoly pricing power.

Elasticity ZoneConditionTotal Revenue RuleExamples
Elastic|Ed| > 1P↑ → TR↓ ; P↓ → TR↑Luxury goods, dining out
Unit Elastic|Ed| = 1TR unchangedThe midpoint benchmark
Inelastic|Ed| < 1P↑ → TR↑ ; P↓ → TR↓Gasoline, insulin

Ed = (%ΔQd) / (%ΔP) L = (P − MC)/P Higher Lerner Index = more market power

Module 03 · OpenStax Micro Ch.12

🏭 Externalities & Pigouvian Tax

Compare private and social costs/benefits and correct market failure.

ConceptMeaning
Negative ExternalityMSC > MPC (pollution, congestion)
Positive ExternalityMSB > MPB (education, vaccination)
Pigouvian TaxTax equal to marginal external cost
Pigouvian SubsidySubsidy equal to marginal external benefit
Corrective Policy
For a negative externality: Optimal tax = MEC at Q_social For a positive externality: Optimal subsidy = MEB at Q_social
📗 Practical Use
Set the corrective tax until the private equilibrium equals the social optimum. This is how carbon taxes and congestion pricing are justified.

🏛️ Public Goods & Free Rider

Why private markets underprovide public goods and how governments respond.

Good TypeExcludableRivalExample
Private GoodYesYesFood, shoes
Public GoodNoNoNational defense, lighthouse
Club GoodYesNoNetflix, toll road

Module 05 · OpenStax Micro Ch.9

🏢 Monopoly

Single-firm market power, MR=MC output, and deadweight loss.

Monopoly Logic
Demand: P = a − bQ MR: a − 2bQ Set MR = MC → find Q* Use demand curve to find monopoly price P*
Locate MR=MC to find profit-maximizing quantity.
Move up to demand to read monopoly price.
Compare with competitive output where P=MC.
Identify DWL between monopoly and competitive quantities.
📗 Key Insight
MR lies below demand because the monopolist must lower price on all units to sell one more unit.
Module 06 · Perloff & Brander Ch.13

🤝 Cournot Duopoly

Two firms simultaneously choose quantities in an oligopoly.

Symmetric Cournot
q* = (a − c)/(3b) Q* = 2(a − c)/(3b) P* = a − bQ*
📗 Interpretation
Each firm restricts output relative to competition but produces more than a monopolist would allow.
Module 07 · Perloff & Brander Ch.13

👑 Stackelberg Leader-Follower

Sequential quantity competition with first-mover advantage.

Stackelberg
Leader chooses first: qL = (a − c)/(2b) Follower best-responds: qF = (a − c)/(4b)
📗 First-Mover Advantage
The leader commits to a larger output, forcing the follower to back down. Credible commitment is everything.
Module 08 · OpenStax Micro Ch.10

🤫 Collusion & Cartels

Cooperation among firms and the temptation to cheat.

ICC for Cartel Stability
δ* = (Defect − Coop)/(Defect − Punish) If δ ≥ δ* → cartel stable If δ < δ* → cheating dominates
⚠️ Why Cartels Break
Each firm wants the cartel price to stay high — but also wants to secretly undercut rivals and steal market share.

Module 21 · OpenStax Macro Ch.11

📊 AD-AS Model

Aggregate Demand–Aggregate Supply: the workhorse short-run macro model.

OpenStax Macro 3e Ch.11
PropertyDetails
DifficultyIntermediate
ChartAD curve, SRAS curve, LRAS vertical line
Use CaseRecessionary gap, inflationary gap, supply shocks, stabilization policy

Inputs / Controls

InputMeaningEffect of Increase
Autonomous SpendingBaseline planned spendingAD shifts right
Government SpendingFiscal stimulusAD shifts right
Money SupplyNominal money stockAD shifts right via lower rates
Input CostEconomy-wide production costSRAS shifts left
Supply ShockTemporary shock to productionPositive → SRAS left; negative → SRAS right
Potential Output (Yp)Full-employment GDPMoves LRAS
Conceptual Logic
AD: total planned spending at each price level SRAS: output firms are willing to produce at each price level in the short run LRAS: full-employment output (potential GDP) Recessionary gap: Y < Yp Inflationary gap: Y > Yp
Start at long-run equilibrium where AD, SRAS, and LRAS intersect.
Increase government spending to simulate expansionary fiscal policy.
Raise input cost to simulate oil shocks or supply chain disruptions.
Compare short-run vs long-run when the economy self-corrects.
⚠️ Stagflation Warning
A negative supply shock causes both higher inflation and lower output. Demand-side policy alone cannot fix both at the same time.
Module 22 · OpenStax Macro Ch.13

📉 Phillips Curve

The short-run inflation–unemployment tradeoff and the long-run natural rate.

InputMeaning
Natural Rate (u*)Long-run unemployment benchmark
Expected Inflation (πe)Shifts the short-run Phillips curve
Slope Parameter (β)Strength of tradeoff
Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve
π = πe − β(u − u*) Short run: downward sloping Long run: vertical at u*
📗 Interpretation
If policymakers push unemployment below the natural rate, inflation accelerates. In the long run, expectations adjust and the tradeoff disappears.
Module 23 · OpenStax Macro Ch.7

📈 Solow Growth Model

Capital accumulation, depreciation, savings, population growth, and technology.

Core Solow Relationships
Y = A·K^α·L^(1−α) Δk = s·f(k) − (n+δ)k Steady state: s·f(k*) = (n+δ)k*
DriverLong-Run Effect
Higher savings rateRaises level of output, not long-run growth rate
Higher population growthLowers steady-state capital per worker
Higher depreciationLowers steady-state capital
Higher technology (A)Raises output and long-run per-capita growth
📗 Key Insight
Only technological progress can sustain permanent increases in output per worker over time.
Module 24 · OpenStax Macro Ch.18

🎯 Taylor Rule

A simple benchmark for central bank interest rate decisions.

Taylor Rule
i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(output gap) where r* = neutral real rate, π* = inflation target
📗 How to Use
Raise inflation above target and the recommended policy rate rises. Increase a negative output gap and the recommended rate falls.

🏛️ Fiscal Multiplier

Government spending, tax, and balanced-budget multipliers.

Multiplier Set
